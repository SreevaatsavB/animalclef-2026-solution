\title{Ensemble of Global Embeddings and Local Feature Matching for Wildlife Re-Identification Clustering}

\author[1]{Sreevaatsav Bavana}[
  email=bavanasreevaatsav1@gmail.com,
]
\address[1]{Independent Researcher}

\begin{abstract}
We describe a clustering-based pipeline for wildlife individual re-identification
that ranked 9th (ARI 0.471) in the AnimalCLEF 2026 competition out of 283
participants. The task requires grouping unlabeled images of the same individual
across four species --- Eurasian lynx, fire salamander, loggerhead sea turtle,
and Texas horned lizard --- without any known identity set. Our approach fuses
dual global embeddings (MiewID v3 and MegaDescriptor, the latter fine-tuned
per-species with ArcFace) with three local feature matchers (SIFT/RootSIFT,
KAZE, ALIKED+LightGlue) into a weighted per-species similarity matrix, which
drives AgglomerativeClustering with thresholds calibrated via Adjusted Mutual
Information (AMI) grid-search on training data. Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor on
CzechLynx (42k images) with ArcFace was the single largest gain, raising Lynx
training AMI from 0.26 to 0.66. We also show that AMI-based threshold calibration
consistently outperforms ARI-based calibration despite ARI being the competition
metric, and document several failed ideas that are hopefully useful to others.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AnimalCLEF 2026 is a wildlife re-identification task framed as clustering. Given
about 2,400 unlabeled test images across four species --- LynxID2025,
SalamanderID2025, SeaTurtleID2022, and TexasHornedLizards --- the goal is to
group images of the same individual together. There is no known identity set and
the number of clusters is unknown. Performance is measured by Adjusted Rand Index
(ARI)~\cite{picek2025animalclef}.

This differs from standard re-ID: without known identities at test time, you
cannot train a classification head. Texas Horned Lizards have no training split
at all, making them fully zero-shot.

We combine frozen global embeddings with species-specifically fine-tuned ones,
add local feature matching on top, and cluster with agglomerative clustering
using per-species thresholds. The useful parts turned out to be: which local
features work on biological texture, why fine-tuning on CzechLynx matters so
much for Lynx, and why calibrating thresholds with AMI rather than ARI gives
better results even though ARI is the actual metric.

\section{Method}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Pipeline}

For each pair of test images $(i, j)$ we compute:

\begin{equation}
  s(i,j) = m_w \cdot \cos(f_m^i, f_m^j)
          + g_w \cdot \cos(f_g^i, f_g^j)
          + \sum_{k} w_k \cdot \ell_k(i,j)
\end{equation}

where $f_m$ are MiewID embeddings, $f_g$ are MegaDescriptor embeddings, and
$\ell_k$ are local match scores from each extractor. Distance is $1 - s(i,j)$,
fed into AgglomerativeClustering with average linkage and a per-species threshold.
To avoid $O(N^2)$ local matching, we first select the top-$K=100$ candidates per
image using \texttt{torch.cdist} on global embeddings, then only run local
matching on those pairs.

\subsection{Global Models}

\textbf{MiewID v3} (\texttt{conservationxlabs/miewid-msv3}) is an EfficientNetV2-RW-M
trained on 49 wildlife species producing 2152-dim L2-normalized embeddings. We
use it frozen with TTA (original + horizontal flip, averaged and renormalized).

\textbf{MegaDescriptor-T-224} (\texttt{BVRA/MegaDescriptor-T-224}) is a Swin-T
backbone producing 768-dim embeddings, pretrained on
WildlifeReID-10k~\cite{pecot2024wildlifereid}. We fine-tune it per-species
using ArcFace loss (margin$=0.25$, scale$=16$) and AdamW (lr$=1\text{e-}4$).
For Lynx, we combine the competition training images with 42k images from
CzechLynx~\cite{picek2025czechlynx}. For Salamander we only have 1,388 training
images for 587 identities --- nowhere near enough, and the fine-tuned model ends
up with weight 0 in calibration. THL has no training data at all and falls back
to pretrained MegaDescriptor-L-384.

\subsection{Local Features}

Biffi et al.~\cite{biffi2025thl} showed that Texas Horned Lizards can be
individually identified from ventral spot patterns alone using
HotSpotter~\cite{crall2013hotspotter}, achieving 94\% accuracy on photo-ID.
HotSpotter works by matching local patch descriptors around distinctive spots
and markings --- the same principle that motivates our local feature pipeline.
The spot patterns on fire salamanders, the scute arrangements on sea turtle heads,
and the scale rows on Texas Horned Lizards are all stable, individually distinctive
markings that global embeddings can average away but local descriptors can lock
onto. We use three extractors, each running on the raw image.

Keypoints that land on background pixels are discarded after extraction using
precomputed SAM3+YOLO segmentation masks. After, not before: replacing background
pixels with white before extraction creates a sharp boundary edge that SIFT
treats as a high-response region, flooding the match with pose-dependent
boundary keypoints (V1.2, ARI dropped from 0.307 to 0.263).

Figure~\ref{fig:matches} shows local matching on same-individual vs.\
different-individual pairs.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{same_individual.png}
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{diff_individual.png}
  \caption{Top: keypoint matches for a same-individual pair --- dense and
    geometrically consistent. Bottom: a different-individual pair --- sparse and
    scattered. Match score is $1 - \exp(-n_\text{matches}/20)$.}
  \label{fig:matches}
\end{figure}

\textbf{SIFT + RootSIFT.} Standard SIFT followed by the RootSIFT
transform~\cite{arandjelovic2012three}: L1-normalize descriptors then take
element-wise square root. This converts L2 distance to Hellinger distance and
gave a free +12.5\% improvement (V2.2). One hard-won lesson: do not apply
RootSIFT to KAZE. KAZE descriptors are signed and the square root of negative
values gives NaN, silently zeroing all matches.

\textbf{KAZE.} Non-linear scale-space detector with 64-dim M-SURF descriptors.
Finds stable keypoints in dense biological texture where SIFT's Gaussian pyramid
blurs detail. Added +7\% across V2.3b through V2.5.

\textbf{ALIKED + LightGlue.} ALIKED-n16~\cite{zhao2023aliked} paired with the
LightGlue GNN matcher~\cite{lindenberger2023lightglue}. Most useful for Lynx
(weight 0.35), where SAM3 has zero coverage --- IR camera-trap images don't
segment cleanly, so SIFT/KAZE are noisier there.

\subsection{Threshold Calibration}

For each species with training data we grid-search over ensemble weights and the
clustering threshold, maximizing AMI against ground-truth labels. ARI is the
competition metric but we do not use it for calibration: ARI rewards large
clusters, which pushes thresholds too high and causes systematic over-merging.
When we tried ARI calibration (V4.0-ARI), Lynx collapsed to 8 clusters from 946
images. AMI penalizes impure clusters and finds much more sensible thresholds.

\textbf{Fine-tuning impact by species.} Pretrained MegaDescriptor was useless for
Lynx --- calibration assigned it weight 0.00 in V4.0. After fine-tuning on

dropped from 0.40 to 0.10. Training AMI went from 0.26 to 0.66. The 42k CzechLynx

would not have been enough. SeaTurtle improved modestly. Salamander was unchanged
--- the fine-tuned model was ignored entirely by calibration.

\section{What Didn't Work}
\label{sec:failures}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

About 30 submissions in total; roughly half regressed. Table~\ref{tab:regressions}
lists the notable ones and the descriptions below cover the non-obvious failures.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Experiments that regressed. $\Delta$ is relative to the best score at the time.}
  \label{tab:regressions}
  \begin{tabular}{llrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Version} & \textbf{Experiment} & \textbf{ARI} & \textbf{$\Delta$} \\
    \midrule
    V1.2       & SAM3 white-background replacement     & 0.263 & $-0.043$ \\
    V2.0       & LNBNN scoring                         & 0.107 & $-0.218$ \\
    V2.4       & Alpha Query Expansion on MiewID       & 0.356 & $-0.021$ \\
    V2.7       & XFeat replacing KAZE                  & 0.373 & $-0.082$ \\
    V3.3       & SuperPoint as 4th local extractor     & 0.472 & $-0.007$ \\
    V4.0-ARI   & ARI-calibrated thresholds             & 0.418 & $-0.061$ \\
    V5.0       & Isotonic score calibration            & 0.348 & $-0.138$ \\
    V5.2--V5.4 & Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor-L-384      & 0.420--0.479 & varies \\
    V5.6       & HDBSCAN clustering                    & 0.214 & $-0.303$ \\
    V5.7       & k-Reciprocal Re-ranking               & 0.491 & $-0.027$ \\
    V5.9       & Fine-tuning both MiewID + MegaDesc    & 0.491 & $-0.027$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{SAM3 white-background replacement (V1.2, ARI 0.263).}
Replacing background pixels with white before running SIFT dropped the score from
0.307 to 0.263. SIFT is edge-based, and the animal/white boundary becomes a strong
detector response --- those boundary keypoints are pose-dependent, not
individual-discriminative, and they pollute every match. Running SIFT on the raw
image and discarding keypoints that fall on background pixels afterward avoids this.

\textbf{LNBNN scoring (V2.0, ARI 0.107).}
LNBNN is HotSpotter's matching score and measures how much closer a descriptor is
to its nearest match than to the background distribution. That background pool in
our pipeline is the top-$K=100$ candidate images --- all near-duplicate animals
from the same species --- so background distance ends up nearly identical to match
distance for almost every descriptor. Nearly all LNBNN deltas are zero or negative,
local scores collapse to zero, and the ensemble falls back to global cosine
similarity with badly miscalibrated thresholds. Score dropped to 0.107.
LNBNN needs a large diverse database; it does not transfer to a curated shortlist.

\textbf{Alpha Query Expansion (V2.4, ARI 0.356).}
AQE on MiewID embeddings dropped 5.6\%. Salamander lost 105 clusters (different
individuals merged) and Lynx gained 71 (same individuals split apart). Per-pair
agreement with V2.3c was 99.2--100\%, so the changes were real --- just in the
wrong direction. MiewID embeddings are already well-structured; smoothing them with
neighborhood averages removes the fine-grained variation the clustering depends on.

\textbf{XFeat replacing KAZE (V2.7, ARI 0.373).}
XFeat (CVPR 2024) is a learned GPU-accelerated descriptor. Score regressed 18\%.
KAZE uses anisotropic diffusion and finds stable keypoints in dense biological
texture; XFeat was trained on human-made scenes and its descriptor distribution
does not transfer. Calibration assigned XFeat weight 0.05--0.15 versus KAZE's
0.15--0.25, and the Salamander threshold shifted from 0.25 to 0.45, causing
massive over-merging (564~$\to$~206 clusters). Classical descriptors can outperform
learned ones when the domain gap is this large.

\textbf{SuperPoint + LightGlue (V3.3, ARI 0.472).}
SuperPoint as a fourth local extractor regressed from V3.2.2 (0.479). SuperPoint
detects corners and junctions --- structural features that are consistent across
individuals of the same species. A salamander's head corner looks the same on
every salamander. Calibration assigned it weight $= 0$ for Lynx, but its presence
in the grid shifted the thresholds for other species enough to hurt.

\textbf{Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor-L-384 (V5.2--V5.4).}
Upgrading from T-224 (Swin-T, 28M params) to L-384 (Swin-L, 197M params) failed
twice. The conservative run (V5.2, LR $3\text{e-}5$, 3 epochs) barely moved from
pretrained. The aggressive run (V5.4, LLRD, LR $1\text{e-}3$, 6 epochs) produced
good standalone AMI (+0.42 for Lynx over pretrained) but regressed on the leaderboard
because we simultaneously expanded the calibration grid --- MegaDesc weight up to
0.80 --- which overfit to training distribution. Lynx ended up with 18 clusters on
test instead of $\sim$40. The model and the calibration grid should not have been
changed at the same time.

\textbf{Isotonic score calibration (V5.0, ARI 0.348).}
Isotonic regression to normalize ensemble scores to $[0,1]$ before thresholding
compressed all species' score distributions into the same range, so every species
hit the threshold ceiling at the same time. Salamander dropped from 492 clusters
to a much lower number. Score distributions fitted on training data do not transfer
to test when embeddings are this domain-specific. Removing it in V5.1 recovered
0.17 ARI immediately.

\textbf{HDBSCAN clustering (V5.6, ARI 0.214).}
Replacing AgglomerativeClustering with HDBSCAN dropped the score to 0.214.
HDBSCAN marks low-density points as noise and assigns them singleton labels.
Animal re-ID has a heavy long tail --- many individuals appear in only 1--2 test
images --- so the data is inherently sparse. HDBSCAN marked most images as noise,
each becoming its own cluster. Density-based clustering is the wrong choice when
the rarest classes are singletons by construction.

\textbf{k-Reciprocal Re-ranking (V5.7, ARI 0.491).}
k-Reciprocal Re-ranking (Zhong et al., CVPR 2017) is a standard post-processing
step in single-query retrieval. Applied to our symmetric all-pairs similarity
matrix it regressed. The method relies on the asymmetry between a single query and
a fixed gallery; in pairwise clustering that asymmetry does not exist. The
Jaccard--cosine combination just diluted our calibrated ensemble signal.

\textbf{Fine-tuning both global models (V5.9, ARI 0.491).}
With both MiewID and MegaDescriptor fine-tuned per-species and strong training AMI,
the ensemble still dropped. Two fine-tuned models are more correlated than one
fine-tuned and one frozen: both see the same training images and converge toward
similar representations. MiewID's value in the ensemble comes partly from being
frozen on a broad 49-species pretraining --- it provides a view the per-species
fine-tuned model does not have. Fine-tuning both collapses that diversity.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Our final submission (ARI 0.517 public, 0.471 private) combines MiewID v3 (frozen),
a fine-tuned MegaDescriptor-T-224, RootSIFT, KAZE, and ALIKED+LightGlue, with
AMI-calibrated per-species thresholds. The biggest single takeaway is that ArcFace
fine-tuning on a large external dataset transforms a useless pretrained feature
into the dominant signal --- CzechLynx took Lynx from essentially random to 0.66
training AMI. Using AMI for threshold calibration rather than ARI was consistently
better despite ARI being the evaluation metric, and SAM3 mask filtering works best
applied after extraction, not before it. Salamander remains unsolved: 1,388 images
for 587 identities is not enough for any fine-tuning approach we tried, and no
large-scale public fire salamander re-ID dataset exists to fill the gap.
