\title{Ensemble of Global Embeddings and Local Feature Matching for Wildlife Re-Identification Clustering}

\author[1]{Sreevaatsav Bavana}[
  email=bavanasreevaatsav1@gmail.com,
]
\address[1]{Independent Researcher}

\begin{abstract}
We describe a clustering-based pipeline for wildlife individual re-identification
that ranked 9th (ARI 0.471) in the AnimalCLEF 2026 competition out of 283
participants. The task requires grouping unlabeled images of the same individual
across four species --- Eurasian lynx, fire salamander, loggerhead sea turtle,
and Texas horned lizard --- without any known identity set. We combine two global
embedding models (MiewID v3 and MegaDescriptor, the latter fine-tuned per-species
with ArcFace) with three local feature matchers (SIFT/RootSIFT, KAZE,
ALIKED+LightGlue) into a weighted per-species similarity matrix, then cluster
with AgglomerativeClustering using thresholds calibrated via AMI grid-search on
training data. Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor on CzechLynx (42k images) was the
single largest gain, raising Lynx training AMI from 0.26 to 0.66. We also found
that calibrating thresholds with AMI consistently beats ARI-based calibration
despite ARI being the competition metric. We include a table of regressions ---
about half our submissions went backwards --- in case that is useful to others.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AnimalCLEF 2026 is a wildlife re-identification task framed as clustering. Given
about 2,400 unlabeled test images across four species --- LynxID2025,
SalamanderID2025, SeaTurtleID2022, and TexasHornedLizards --- the goal is to
group images of the same individual together. There is no known identity set,
the number of clusters is unknown, and performance is measured by Adjusted Rand
Index (ARI)~\cite{picek2025animalclef}.

Unlike standard re-ID, there are no known identities at test time, so you cannot
train a classification head --- the only lever is how good your embeddings are and
where you set the clustering threshold. Texas Horned Lizards have no training data
at all, making them fully zero-shot.

We combined frozen pretrained global embeddings with fine-tuned ones and added
local feature matching on top. The three things worth writing about are: which
local descriptors actually help on biological texture and why, why external data
(CzechLynx) was so important for Lynx, and why AMI gives better calibration than
ARI even when ARI is what you are evaluated on.

\section{Related Work}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The 2025 edition of AnimalCLEF~\cite{adam2025animalclef} was an open-set
identification task: each query image had to be matched against a known gallery
and either assigned to an existing individual or flagged as a new one.
Performance was measured by BAKS and BAUS. The 2026 edition removes the gallery
entirely --- there are no known identities at test time, and the task is pure
clustering evaluated by ARI. This changes the problem substantially.

The top two 2025 solutions~\cite{pakhomov2025animalclef,semenova2025animalclef}
both follow a global+local ensemble structure similar to ours: MegaDescriptor or
MiewID for global embeddings, ALIKED+LightGlue for local matching, and a
per-species threshold for the open-set decision. Both build on
WildFusion~\cite{cermak2024wildfusion}, which fuses global cosine similarity
with local keypoint scores via isotonic calibration. Semenova~\cite{semenova2025animalclef}
additionally applied k-reciprocal re-ranking~\cite{zhong2017reranking}, both of
which gave gains in the retrieval setting. We tried both for clustering and both
regressed --- isotonic calibration because training and test score distributions
do not transfer when the task is fully unsupervised, and k-reciprocal re-ranking
because it is built around a query-vs-gallery asymmetry that does not exist in
symmetric all-pairs clustering.
Our specific contributions over the 2025 approaches are: KAZE for dense
biological texture matching, the RootSIFT transform, AMI-based threshold
calibration, ArcFace fine-tuning on CzechLynx~\cite{picek2025czechlynx}, and
extending to a fourth species (Texas Horned Lizards) under zero-shot conditions.

\section{Method}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Pipeline}

For each pair of test images $(i, j)$ we compute:

\begin{equation}
  s(i,j) = m_w \cdot \cos(f_m^i, f_m^j)
          + g_w \cdot \cos(f_g^i, f_g^j)
          + \sum_{k} w_k \cdot \ell_k(i,j)
\end{equation}

where $f_m$ are MiewID embeddings, $f_g$ are MegaDescriptor embeddings, and
$\ell_k$ are local match scores. Distance is $1 - s(i,j)$, fed into
AgglomerativeClustering with average linkage. To keep local matching tractable
we first preselect the top-$K=100$ candidates per image using
\texttt{torch.cdist} on global embeddings and only run local matching on those
pairs, avoiding the $O(N^2)$ full pairwise cost.

\subsection{Global Models}

\textbf{MiewID v3}~\cite{kholiavchenko2024miewid} (\texttt{conservationxlabs/miewid-msv3})
is an EfficientNetV2-RW-M backbone trained on 49 wildlife species, producing 2152-dim
L2-normalized embeddings. We use it frozen throughout, with TTA (original + horizontal
flip, averaged and renormalized).

\textbf{MegaDescriptor-T-224} (\texttt{BVRA/MegaDescriptor-T-224}) is a Swin-T
backbone producing 768-dim embeddings, pretrained on
WildlifeReID-10k~\cite{pecot2024wildlifereid}. We fine-tune it per-species with
ArcFace loss (margin$=0.25$, scale$=16$) and AdamW (lr$=1\text{e-}4$). For Lynx,
the competition training images are merged with 42k images from
CzechLynx~\cite{picek2025czechlynx}. For Salamander we only had 1,388 images
across 587 identities --- not enough, and calibration ended up giving the
fine-tuned model weight 0. THL has no training data; it falls back to the
pretrained MegaDescriptor-L-384.

\subsection{Local Features}

Biffi et al.~\cite{biffi2025thl} showed that individual Texas Horned Lizards can
be identified from ventral spot patterns alone using HotSpotter~\cite{crall2013hotspotter},
achieving 94\% accuracy. HotSpotter matches local patch descriptors around
distinctive spots --- the same idea behind our local feature pipeline. The scale
rows on Texas Horned Lizards, yellow spot patterns on fire salamanders, and scute
arrangements on sea turtle heads are all stable across re-sightings and carry
individual identity in a way that global average-pooling can miss. We run three
extractors, all on the raw image.

Keypoints that fall on background pixels are discarded after extraction using
precomputed SAM3+YOLO segmentation masks. The order matters: replacing the
background with white before extraction creates a hard edge at the animal boundary
that SIFT responds to strongly, filling the match with border keypoints that vary
with pose rather than identity. This cost us 14\% in V1.2 (0.307 $\to$ 0.263)
before we caught it.

Figure~\ref{fig:matches} shows what matching looks like on same vs.\ different
individual pairs.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{same_individual.png}
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{diff_individual.png}
  \caption{Top: keypoint matches for a same-individual pair --- dense and
    geometrically consistent. Bottom: a different-individual pair --- sparse and
    scattered. Match score is $1 - \exp(-n_\text{matches}/20)$.}
  \label{fig:matches}
\end{figure}

\textbf{SIFT + RootSIFT.} Standard SIFT followed by the RootSIFT
transform~\cite{arandjelovic2012three}: L1-normalize descriptors, then take
element-wise square root. This converts L2 distance to Hellinger distance and
was +12.5\% over raw SIFT for free (V2.2). Do not apply it to KAZE --- KAZE
descriptors are signed and the square root of a negative is NaN, silently
zeroing all matches.

\textbf{KAZE.} Non-linear scale-space detector with 64-dim M-SURF descriptors.
It finds stable keypoints in dense biological texture where SIFT's Gaussian pyramid
just blurs the fine structure away. Added +7\% across V2.3b through V2.5.

\textbf{ALIKED + LightGlue.} ALIKED-n16~\cite{zhao2023aliked} paired with the
LightGlue GNN matcher~\cite{lindenberger2023lightglue}. Most useful for Lynx
(weight 0.35) --- the IR camera-trap images in LynxID2025 do not segment cleanly
under SAM3, which makes SIFT and KAZE noisier there.

\subsection{Threshold Calibration}

For each species with training data we grid-search over ensemble weights and the
clustering threshold, maximizing AMI against ground-truth labels. We do not use
ARI for calibration even though that is the competition metric. ARI rewards large
clusters, which pushes thresholds too high: when we tried ARI calibration
(V4.0-ARI), Lynx collapsed from 946 images down to 8 clusters. AMI counts impure
clusters as errors, so it tends to find thresholds that actually reflect the
true granularity of the data.

\textbf{Fine-tuning impact.} Before fine-tuning, pretrained MegaDescriptor had
weight 0 for Lynx in V4.0 --- calibration gave it nothing because it was not
discriminative. After training on CzechLynx it became the dominant signal: weight
0.50 in V5.1 while MiewID dropped from 0.40 to 0.10. Training AMI went from 0.26
to 0.66. The 42k CzechLynx images~\cite{picek2025czechlynx} were what made this
work; fine-tuning on the 2,957 competition images alone would not have moved the
needle. For SeaTurtle the improvement was modest. For Salamander the fine-tuned
model got weight 0 again --- 1,388 training images across 587 identities is simply
not enough.

\section{What Didn't Work}
\label{sec:failures}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

About 30 submissions in total, roughly half of which regressed.
Table~\ref{tab:regressions} lists the notable ones; below we explain the failures
where the cause was not obvious.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Experiments that regressed. $\Delta$ is relative to the best score at the time.}
  \label{tab:regressions}
  \begin{tabular}{llrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Version} & \textbf{Experiment} & \textbf{ARI} & \textbf{$\Delta$} \\
    \midrule
    V1.2       & SAM3 white-background replacement     & 0.263 & $-0.043$ \\
    V2.0       & LNBNN scoring                         & 0.107 & $-0.218$ \\
    V2.4       & Alpha Query Expansion on MiewID       & 0.356 & $-0.021$ \\
    V2.7       & XFeat replacing KAZE                  & 0.373 & $-0.082$ \\
    V3.3       & SuperPoint as 4th local extractor     & 0.472 & $-0.007$ \\
    V4.0-ARI   & ARI-calibrated thresholds             & 0.418 & $-0.061$ \\
    V5.0       & Isotonic score calibration            & 0.348 & $-0.138$ \\
    V5.2--V5.4 & Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor-L-384      & 0.420--0.479 & varies \\
    V5.6       & HDBSCAN clustering                    & 0.214 & $-0.303$ \\
    V5.7       & k-Reciprocal Re-ranking               & 0.491 & $-0.027$ \\
    V5.9       & Fine-tuning both MiewID + MegaDesc    & 0.491 & $-0.027$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{SAM3 white-background replacement (V1.2, ARI 0.263).}
Replacing background pixels with white before SIFT extraction cost 14\%.
SIFT fires on edges, and painting the background white draws a sharp edge all
around the animal --- those border keypoints change with pose and carry nothing
about identity. The fix was to run SIFT on the untouched image and drop any
keypoint whose pixel coordinate falls on a background mask pixel afterward.

\textbf{LNBNN scoring (V2.0, ARI 0.107).}
LNBNN measures how much closer a descriptor is to its nearest match than to the
background distribution. In HotSpotter this works because the background pool is a
large varied database. Here the background pool is the top-$K=100$ candidates,
which are all near-duplicate animals from the same species. Background distance
ends up nearly equal to match distance for most descriptors, nearly all deltas
go to zero or below, and local scores collapse. The ensemble then falls back to
global cosine with thresholds that were calibrated for a mix of global and local
signal --- which is why the score did not just stay flat but actually crashed to
0.107.

\textbf{Alpha Query Expansion (V2.4, ARI 0.356).}
AQE on MiewID dropped 5.6\%. Salamander lost 105 clusters (over-merged) and Lynx
gained 71 (over-split). We checked per-pair agreement against V2.3c and it was
99.2--100\%, meaning the changes were real, not noise. MiewID embeddings were
already well-placed for this task; averaging in neighbors smeared away the
fine-grained separation we needed.

\textbf{XFeat replacing KAZE (V2.7, ARI 0.373).}
XFeat (CVPR 2024) is a GPU-accelerated learned descriptor. Score dropped 18\%.
KAZE was trained on patterns in nature; XFeat was trained on human-made scenes.
The descriptor distributions are incompatible enough that calibration assigned
XFeat weight 0.05--0.15 compared to KAZE's 0.15--0.25, and the Salamander
threshold shifted from 0.25 to 0.45, merging 564 clusters down to 206.

\textbf{SuperPoint + LightGlue (V3.3, ARI 0.472).}
SuperPoint as a fourth extractor regressed from V3.2.2 (0.479). SuperPoint
detects corners and junctions --- structural features that repeat across every
individual of the same species. A fire salamander's head junction looks the same
on every fire salamander. Calibration gave it weight 0 for Lynx, but having it
in the grid at all shifted the other thresholds enough to hurt.

\textbf{Fine-tuning MegaDescriptor-L-384 (V5.2--V5.4).}
Moving from T-224 (Swin-T, 28M params) to L-384 (Swin-L, 197M params) failed
twice. The conservative run (V5.2, LR $3\text{e-}5$, 3 epochs) barely shifted
from pretrained. The aggressive run (V5.4, LLRD, LR $1\text{e-}3$, 6 epochs)
gave genuinely good per-species AMI on training data (+0.42 for Lynx), but we
also widened the calibration grid at the same time --- MegaDesc weight up to
0.80 --- and that overfit. Lynx dropped to 18 clusters on the test set. We should
not have changed the model and the calibration grid in the same submission.

\textbf{Isotonic score calibration (V5.0, ARI 0.348).}
Isotonic regression on training similarity scores before thresholding squeezed all
four species into the same $[0,1]$ range, which meant they all hit the threshold
ceiling at once. Salamander went from 492 clusters to far fewer. The training and
test score distributions are different enough that a calibration fitted on one
does not carry over to the other. Removing it recovered 0.17 ARI in the next
submission.

\textbf{HDBSCAN clustering (V5.6, ARI 0.214).}
Swapping AgglomerativeClustering for HDBSCAN collapsed the score to 0.214.
HDBSCAN labels sparse points as noise; each noise point becomes its own cluster.
Re-ID datasets have many individuals who appear only once or twice, so large parts
of the data are inherently sparse, and HDBSCAN flagged most images as noise.

\textbf{k-Reciprocal Re-ranking (V5.7, ARI 0.491).}
k-Reciprocal Re-ranking~\cite{zhong2017reranking} is designed for single-query
retrieval, where there is an asymmetry between one query and a fixed gallery.
Our task is symmetric all-pairs clustering --- that asymmetry does not exist here,
and the Jaccard--cosine reweighting just diluted the signal we already had.

\textbf{Fine-tuning both global models (V5.9, ARI 0.491).}
Even with strong per-species training AMI, fine-tuning both MiewID and
MegaDescriptor together still regressed. When both models are trained on the same
species data they converge toward similar representations and the ensemble loses
diversity. MiewID's usefulness in V5.1 comes partly from it being frozen on a
broad multi-species pretraining --- it sees things the per-species fine-tuned
model does not. Once you fine-tune both, that complementarity is gone.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Our final submission (ARI 0.517 public, 0.471 private) combined frozen MiewID v3,
fine-tuned MegaDescriptor-T-224, RootSIFT, KAZE, and ALIKED+LightGlue, with
AMI-calibrated thresholds per species. The Lynx result captures the main lesson:
fine-tuning on a large external dataset (CzechLynx, 42k images) took a model that
was contributing nothing to the ensemble and made it the dominant feature. Without
that external data, MegaDescriptor would have stayed at weight 0. The AMI vs.\ ARI
calibration difference was also real and consistent across submissions --- ARI's
bias toward large clusters causes systematic over-merging that AMI avoids. For
SAM3 masks, filtering keypoints after extraction rather than pre-processing the
image is what works. Salamander remains the one species we could not crack ---
1,388 training images for 587 identities is just not enough for fine-tuning to
help, and there is no large public fire salamander dataset we could pull in the
way we pulled CzechLynx for Lynx.
