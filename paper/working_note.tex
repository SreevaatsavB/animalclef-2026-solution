\title{Ensemble of Global Embeddings and Local Feature Matching for Wildlife Re-Identification Clustering}

\author[1]{Sreevaatsav Bavana}[
  email=bavanasreevaatsav1@gmail.com,
]
\address[1]{Independent Researcher}

\begin{abstract}
We describe a clustering-based pipeline for wildlife individual re-identification
that ranked 9th (ARI 0.471) in the AnimalCLEF 2026 competition out of 283 participants.
The task requires grouping unlabeled images of the same individual across four species
--- Eurasian lynx, fire salamander, loggerhead sea turtle, and Texas horned lizard ---
without any known identity set. Our approach fuses dual global embeddings (MiewID v3
and MegaDescriptor, the latter fine-tuned per-species with ArcFace) with three local
feature matchers (SIFT/RootSIFT, KAZE, ALIKED+LightGlue) into a weighted per-species
similarity matrix, which drives AgglomerativeClustering with thresholds calibrated via
Adjusted Mutual Information (AMI) grid-search on training data. Fine-tuning
MegaDescriptor on CzechLynx (42k images) using ArcFace was the single largest
improvement, raising Lynx training AMI from 0.26 to 0.66. We show that AMI-based
threshold calibration consistently outperforms ARI-based calibration despite ARI being
the competition metric. We also document several instructive failures including LNBNN
scoring, alpha query expansion, and SuperPoint local features.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AnimalCLEF 2026 frames wildlife re-identification as an unsupervised clustering
problem over four species: LynxID2025 (946 test images), SalamanderID2025 (689),
SeaTurtleID2022 (500), and TexasHornedLizards (274, zero training split). Given a
set of unlabeled query images, participants must assign cluster labels such that images
of the same individual share a label. Performance is measured by Adjusted Rand Index
(ARI) against ground-truth identity assignments.

This task differs fundamentally from the 2025 edition, which was a closed-set
classification problem with known identities. Clustering is harder: there is no
reference identity set, the number of clusters is unknown, and the evaluation metric
(ARI) is sensitive to both over-merging and over-splitting errors.

Our solution builds on three ideas: (1) combining frozen pretrained global embeddings
with species-specifically fine-tuned ones to maximise embedding discriminativeness;
(2) complementing global similarity with traditional and learned local feature matching
to resolve near-duplicate pairs; and (3) calibrating per-species clustering thresholds
using AMI on training data rather than the competition metric ARI, which we show is
systematically biased toward over-merging.

\section{Method}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

For each pair of test images $(i, j)$ we compute a combined similarity score:

\begin{equation}
  s(i,j) = m_w \cdot \cos(f_m^i, f_m^j)
          + g_w \cdot \cos(f_g^i, f_g^j)
          + \sum_{k} w_k \cdot \ell_k(i,j)
\end{equation}

where $f_m$ are MiewID embeddings, $f_g$ are MegaDescriptor embeddings, $\ell_k$ are
per-extractor local match scores, and all weights are calibrated per species. The
distance matrix $d(i,j) = 1 - s(i,j)$ is fed to AgglomerativeClustering with average
linkage.

\subsection{Global Feature Extraction}

\textbf{MiewID v3} (\texttt{conservationxlabs/miewid-msv3}) is an EfficientNetV2-RW-M
backbone trained on 49 wildlife species producing 2152-dim L2-normalized embeddings.
It is used frozen. Test-time augmentation (original + horizontal flip, averaged and
renormalized) is applied.

\textbf{MegaDescriptor-T-224} (\texttt{BVRA/MegaDescriptor-T-224}) is a Swin-T
backbone producing 768-dim embeddings, pretrained on WildlifeReID-10k~\cite{pecot2024wildlifereid}.
We fine-tune it per-species using ArcFace loss (margin$=0.25$, scale$=16$),
AdamW (lr$=1\text{e-}4$, wd$=0.01$), with all parameters trainable from epoch 1.
For Lynx we merge LynxID2025 training images with 42k images from the CzechLynx
dataset~\cite{picek2025czechlynx}; for SeaTurtle we use SeaTurtleID2022 training
images; for Salamander we use SalamanderID2025 training images (only 1,388 images ---
insufficient for meaningful fine-tuning, as confirmed by ablation). Texas Horned
Lizards have no training split and fall back to pretrained MegaDescriptor-L-384.

\subsection{Local Feature Matching}

All local extractors operate on the raw (unsegmented) image. Keypoints that land on
background pixels --- identified using precomputed SAM3+YOLO segmentation masks ---
are discarded post-extraction. Applying masks before extraction degrades performance
by creating strong boundary-artifact keypoints at the animal/background boundary.

\textbf{SIFT + RootSIFT.} Standard SIFT detection followed by the RootSIFT
transform~\cite{arandjelovic2012three}: L1-normalize descriptors then take element-wise
square root. This converts L2 distance to Hellinger distance, which is more
discriminative for gradient-histogram descriptors. Match score:
$\ell = 1 - \exp(-n_{\text{matches}}/20)$.

\textbf{KAZE.} Non-linear (anisotropic diffusion) scale-space detector with 64-dim
M-SURF descriptors. Finds stable keypoints in dense biological texture where SIFT's
Gaussian pyramid blurs detail. RootSIFT must \emph{not} be applied to KAZE: KAZE
descriptors are signed, so element-wise square root produces NaN values and silent
zero-match failures.

\textbf{ALIKED + LightGlue.} ALIKED-n16~\cite{zhao2023aliked} learned keypoint
detector paired with the LightGlue attentional GNN matcher~\cite{lindenberger2023lightglue}.
Particularly effective for Lynx (calibrated weight 0.35), which lacks SAM3 segmentation
masks (IR camera-trap images).

For computational efficiency, a GPU-accelerated top-$K=100$ candidate preselection
using \texttt{torch.cdist} on global embeddings precedes local matching, reducing
complexity from $O(N^2)$ to $O(NK)$.

\subsection{Threshold Calibration}

For each species with a training split, we run a grid-search over ensemble weights and
clustering threshold, maximizing AMI against ground-truth identity labels. Despite ARI
being the competition metric, we use AMI as the proxy because ARI rewards large
clusters, causing systematic over-merging when used for calibration
(see Section~\ref{sec:failures}).

\textbf{AMI vs ARI calibration.} Using ARI as the calibration metric (V4.0-ARI)
collapsed Lynx to 8 clusters from 946 images (ARI 0.418), compared to 38 clusters
and ARI 0.486 with AMI calibration. ARI's mathematical structure penalizes splitting
large clusters less than creating impure ones, causing thresholds to systematically
over-merge when used as the calibration objective.

\section{Analysis of Failures}
\label{sec:failures}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We document four instructive failures.

\textbf{LNBNN scoring (V2.0, ARI 0.107).} Replacing BFMatcher with Local Naive Bayes
Nearest Neighbor (HotSpotter-style) scoring caused near-total collapse. With $K=100$
candidate preselection, the background descriptor pool contains $\sim$100k descriptors
from similar-looking animals, making background distance $\approx$ match distance for
most query descriptors, producing near-zero LNBNN scores across the board.

\textbf{Alpha Query Expansion (V2.4, ARI 0.356).} Applying AQE on MiewID embeddings
regressed by 5.6\%. The embedding space was already near-optimal; perturbation pulled
embeddings from different individuals together (Salamander: $-105$ clusters) or apart
(Lynx: $+71$ clusters).

\textbf{SuperPoint + LightGlue (V3.3, ARI 0.472).} SuperPoint detects corners and
junctions --- structural features that are similar across individuals of the same
species, producing false positive matches and threshold drift during calibration.

\textbf{ArcFace fine-tuning of MegaDescriptor-L-384 (V5.2--V5.4).} Conservative
hyperparameters (LR $3\text{e-}5$, 3 epochs) left the model near pretrained weights.
Aggressive tuning (LLRD, LR $1\text{e-}3$, 6 epochs) improved embedding-space AMI
substantially but regressed on the leaderboard, likely due to an expanded calibration
grid (MegaDesc weight up to 0.80) overfitting to the training distribution.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Our best submission (ARI 0.517 public, 0.471 private, 9th of 283) uses a
five-component ensemble of MiewID v3, fine-tuned MegaDescriptor-T-224, RootSIFT,
KAZE, and ALIKED+LightGlue, with AMI-calibrated per-species thresholds. Key findings:
(1) ArcFace fine-tuning on large external data (CzechLynx) transforms an ineffective
pretrained feature into the dominant signal for Lynx; (2) AMI is a better calibration
proxy than ARI even when ARI is the evaluation metric; (3) KAZE outperforms SIFT for
dense biological texture; (4) SAM3 keypoint filtering (not background replacement) is
the correct use of segmentation masks with local feature detectors. Salamander remains
the bottleneck --- 1,388 training images for 587 identities is insufficient for any
fine-tuning approach we tried.
